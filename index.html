2.2 But the Entire Market Is Built Wrong

Here is the problem: virtually every dollar of this market rests on a fundamental architectural error. The onchain token is not the security. It is a pointer to an offchain record maintained by a transfer agent, custodian, or proprietary database. We coined the term “slop tokenization” to describe this phenomenon. The taxonomy is precise:

  • Instruction tokens: Tokens that function as messages to an intermediary requesting a state change in an offchain ledger. The intermediary can ignore the instruction.

  • Receipt tokens: Tokens issued as acknowledgments that an offchain event has occurred. The offchain event is authoritative; the token is a notification.

  • Souvenir tokens: Tokens with no legal operative function whatsoever—connected to underlying value by nothing more than marketing and vibes.

In all three cases, strip away the blockchain and the underlying ownership structure is unchanged. The token adds latency, cost, and complexity without changing the trust model. If the blockchain isn’t performing essential work that the offchain system couldn’t do without it, why do you need a blockchain at all?

2.3 Our Specific Target: Private Market Securities

Our immediate focus is the private market securities segment: venture-stage equity (SAFEs, preferred stock, common stock), SPV interests, and token-equity hybrid instruments. Global venture capital deal volume exceeded $300 billion in 2024, with tens of thousands of SAFE and preferred stock transactions per year, virtually all settling through PDF signatures, wire transfers, and manual cap table management on Carta. Think of us as Carta and Nasdaq Private Market in full symbiosis—but crypto native, onchain, and liquid. For crypto-native companies, where founders and investors already have wallet infrastructure, understand self-custody, and prefer permissionless tooling, we offer a fundamentally better way to raise and manage capital.

2.4 The Pump.fun Pilot: Token-Denominated Community SAFEs

We have developed a novel product for the Pump.fun ecosystem that demonstrates the breadth of what our architecture enables: Community SAFEs, where token holders can convert a project’s own token into equity stakes in the company behind the project. This “post-mine” model inverts the traditional crypto fundraise: the token launches first as a community asset (memecoin, utility token, brand-affinity token) with no reference to equity or SAFEs, and only later is a structured equity financing introduced. Both the investment amount and the valuation cap are denominated in the project’s token, not fiat. Participants bridge their tokens from Solana to Base, pass identity and compliance checks (including nationality verification via zkPassport and KYC/AML screening), and subscribe through a non-custodial smart contract escrow. Upon acceptance, investors receive a cyberCERT embodying the SAFE agreement.

This model addresses a longstanding structural problem in crypto: the gap between token value and enterprise value. Today, equity investors share in a company’s success while token holders are left with a speculative asset with no claim on the business. The Community SAFE changes that—token holders can convert into equity investors through a legally compliant pathway, while the token continues to exist and trade independently. The resulting securities are non-transferable by default and subject to a one-year distribution compliance period under Regulation S. We prepared a comprehensive legal memorandum for this pilot covering Regulation S, Howey analysis, sufficient decentralization, and the structural separation between the token layer and the security layer.

If this model succeeds, every token community with a viable project becomes a potential cyberRaise customer, and the communities bring their own distribution. This is a repeatable, viral go-to-market channel that no competitor can access—because no competitor has the constitutive tokenization architecture required to issue the securities.

3. Our Solution: Securities That Know What They Are

3.1 The Entity-First Architecture

We invert the approach taken by every competing standard. Rather than starting from a token standard and adding compliance features, CyberCorps starts from Delaware General Corporation Law and builds token architecture to satisfy its requirements. The organizing principle is that every component in the system should know its relationship to the others—and should be able to act on that knowledge.

The protocol’s root contract is the cyberCORP—an onchain representation of the corporate entity itself, storing the legal entity’s name, type, jurisdiction, governance information, and officer identities. The cyberCORP knows what entity it is, what securities it has authorized, and what governance rules apply to corporate actions. Securities are issued from this entity contract, creating a formal onchain link between token and issuer that no competing standard provides.

3.2 The Dual-Token Model

3.2.1 cyberCERTs (ERC-721 Stock Ledger Entry Tokens)

Each cyberCERT is an ERC-721 NFT that functions as an entry on the corporation’s onchain stock ledger. It encodes everything a stock ledger entry requires under DGCL §§158 and 219: stockholder name, number/class/series of shares, authorizing officer signatures, purchase price, voting power, valuation cap, transfer restriction legends (satisfying §202), endorsement history, legal agreement URIs, and a fully onchain Base64-encoded SVG visualization. A cyberCERT knows what entity issued it, what series it belongs to, what terms govern that series, what transfer restrictions apply, and what its acquisition date and price were. Anyone buying the security can consult this single onchain source of truth to understand exactly what they are acquiring.

Critically, NFT transfer alone does not change registered ownership—metadata must be explicitly mutated, maintaining the distinction between token possession and registered ownership that corporate law requires.

3.2.2 cyberSCRIPs (ERC-20 Scrip Tokens)

cyberSCRIPs are ERC-20 fungible tokens generated from cyberCERTs via scripification and convertible back via de-scripification. They are the liquid, DeFi-compatible layer—usable in AMMs, vesting protocols, and as collateral—while cyberCERTs remain the canonical ownership record. We frame this through DGCL §155, which authorizes “scrip or warrants” representing fractional shares. Scrip Tokens are explicitly not stock of the corporation—they are conditional claims on underlying cyberCERTs, convertible back into registered stock when conditions are met.

This dual-token model directly resolves the fundamental tension between legal fidelity and DeFi composability. A cyberCERT holder can scripify their certificate, deposit the resulting cyberSCRIPs into a lending protocol as collateral, and the lender obtains token-control of a fungible ERC-20 without admin-key override risk. Think of it as the stablecoin model applied to equity: USDC holders have no rights per se, but qualifying clients of Circle can redeem 1:1 for dollars. We also support what we call “degen mode”—scrip as freely tradeable tokens with no inherent stockholder rights, convertible back into cyberCERTs (and thus real registered shares) by persons who satisfy KYC/AML and other issuer-imposed conditions. Startups can even launch scrip first as something resembling a memecoin and make it convertible into securities later—all on the same protocol. The legal basis is solid: both DGCL §155 and Wyoming BCA §17-16-604 expressly authorize scrip representing fractional shares, and the concept has a long history in corporate law.

3.3 The Constitutive Difference

This is the load-bearing argument. In the CyberCorps model, the corporation’s bylaws and Certificate of Incorporation explicitly designate the onchain system as the official stock ledger under DGCL §224. The token doesn’t merely represent a stock ledger entry; it IS the stock ledger entry. Holding it makes you the stockholder of record. The governing legal instruments fuse the onchain state to the legal reality, so that the smart contract system is performing real legal work, not mirroring an offchain record.

Every competing architecture maintains two ledgers that must be kept in sync, proving the chain isn’t the source of truth. We eliminate the reconciliation layer by making the chain the ledger in the governing documents. Transfer restrictions encoded in our smart contracts are doing real work—they are not redundant to an offchain compliance process, because there is no separate offchain ledger serving as the system of record.

3.4 The Fundamental Move: Signing Authority = Spending Authority

Every other corporate deal system in existence separates two acts that should be one. You sign an agreement (DocuSign, wet ink, PDF) and then, in a separate process, you wire money (bank transfer, escrow agent, closing mechanics). The signing authority and the spending authority are different systems, different credentials, different workflows, different timelines. The gap between signing and funding is where deals fall apart, where counterparty risk lives, where lawyers and escrow agents extract fees, and where weeks of back-and-forth accumulate.

Public key cryptography collapses this gap. An Ethereum wallet uses ECDSA over secp256k1 with Keccak-256—the same cryptographic primitive found in every browser wallet extension. The same private key that can sign a legal agreement (an EIP-712 typed data signature on a CyberAgreement) can also authorize a payment transaction (a stablecoin transfer into an escrow contract). The signing authority IS the spending authority. This is not a UX convenience. It is a fundamental architectural shift that makes an entirely new category of legal-economic operations possible.

We call this design space cybernetic law: the fusion of legal agreements with their economic execution into cryptographically unified workflows. When the same keypair signs the contract and funds the escrow, new things become possible that are structurally impossible in the traditional stack:

  • True atomic sign-and-close: An investor reviews the agreement terms, signs the legal document, and deposits funds into escrow in a single blockchain transaction. There is no gap between “we agreed” and “we performed.” The signature and the funding are the same cryptographic act. The counterparty risk window is zero. No DocuSign, no wire instructions, no escrow agent, no data entry, no reconciliation. In cyberRaise, this is live today: the investor clicks “sign and confirm,” and the ECDSA signature simultaneously binds them to the agreement and commits their capital to the LeXscroW smart contract.

  • Self-executing escrow: Because the escrow contract knows its governing terms (via the CyberAgreementRegistry and Ricardian Triplers), it can automatically release funds to the issuer upon acceptance, or return them to the investor if the expression of interest is not accepted or the round closes without allocation. No escrow agent evaluates conditions and decides whether to release. The conditions are onchain, the evaluation is deterministic, and the execution is automatic. The escrow knows what it’s holding, why, and for whom—and acts accordingly.

  • Tightly coupled agreement/execution flows: The CyberAgreementRegistry anchors legal documents onchain with EIP-712 signatures from all parties. Each security token (cyberCERT) knows which agreement governs it. Each escrow knows which agreement it enforces. Each corporate action traces back to an authorized signing event. The entire chain—from governance authorization through agreement signing through funding through security issuance—is a single auditable sequence of cryptographically linked events, not a stack of PDFs, wire confirmations, and manual cap table entries that must be reconciled after the fact.

  • Entity-to-entity composability: When two cyberCORPs transact—a merger, an investment, a joint venture—both sides’ signing authorities, spending authorities, securities, escrows, and governing documents are part of the same onchain knowledge graph. Because signing = spending on both sides, a cross-entity deal can be structured as an atomic, multi-party escrow where all signatures and all payments resolve simultaneously. No coordinating across multiple law firms, banks, transfer agents, and cap table platforms. The governance approves the deal AND funds it in the same system, because the cryptographic authority to do both is the same key.

This is the deeper design space that CyberCorps opens up—and it goes far beyond fundraising. Dividend distributions that execute automatically when the board resolution is signed. Liquidation waterfalls that read the cap table and pay out in priority order without a paying agent. Conversion mechanics (SAFE to preferred, preferred to common) that trigger atomically when the qualifying event occurs. Rights of first refusal that can be exercised and funded in a single transaction. Onchain corporate governance where a board vote simultaneously authorizes and executes the action it approves. Every corporate action that currently requires a human to bridge the gap between “authorized” and “executed” becomes a candidate for cybernetic automation, because the authority to approve and the authority to act are the same cryptographic credential.

cyberRaise is the first product built on this foundation. The vision is much larger: a full corporate lifecycle—found, fund, manage, trade—where every legal act and every economic act are fused into a single programmable system. We call it CorpFi: DeFi-style corporate finance, where the legal agreements and the money move together.

12. Frequently Asked Questions

We hear smart, skeptical questions regularly. Here are the ones that come up most often, and our honest answers.

“Hasn’t this been tried before? Security token offerings failed in 2017–2019.”

Yes, and for a specific reason: the first generation of security token projects started from the token and tried to retrofit legal compliance. They produced tokens that carried no legal content—no issuer identity, no series terms, no restriction legends, no governing document references—and wrapped them in compliance modules (whitelists, transfer restrictions) that duplicated work the issuer or transfer agent was already doing offchain. The result was an expensive, less-capable mirror of the existing system with no path to DeFi composability. We start from the opposite end: Delaware corporate law first, token architecture second. The CyberCorps protocol doesn’t add a blockchain layer on top of the existing intermediary chain; it replaces the intermediary chain by making the blockchain the legally authoritative stock ledger. That’s a different product, not a retry of the old one.

“Securitize has billions in AUM and institutional relationships with BlackRock. How do you compete with that?”

We don’t compete for the same customers, at least not yet. Securitize serves institutional fund products (like BlackRock’s BUIDL) where the existing custody and transfer agent infrastructure is a feature, not a bug—those clients want the intermediary chain. Our target is crypto-native private companies, venture-stage equity, and token communities where founders and investors already have wallets, understand self-custody, and actively prefer permissionless tooling. That said, Securitize’s architecture has a structural ceiling: its tokens don’t know what they are (an ERC-20 balance that carries no issuer identity, no series terms, no per-share metadata), its DS Protocol retains god-mode admin keys (freeze, force transfer, pause, recover) that make the tokens unsuitable for DeFi collateral, and every new DeFi protocol that wants to interact with a Securitize token must be individually approved by the transfer agent. That approval bottleneck scales linearly with ecosystem adoption. Our architecture eliminates it.

“Doesn’t putting stockholder names on a public blockchain create a privacy disaster?”

This is a real design consideration, and we’ve addressed it at multiple layers. First, the offchain PII requirement is an architectural choice, not a regulatory mandate. Transfer agent law does not require PII to be offchain—the constraint flows from the collision between public blockchain transparency and privacy law. Second, our protocol supports encrypted metadata: investors can encrypt their name and personally identifiable details so that these are only visible to the cyberCORP founder and not recorded in plaintext onchain. Third, pre-IPO cyberCERTs can be soulbound (non-transferable), limiting onchain visibility to the holder’s own wallet and authorized onchain roles. Fourth, the broader ecosystem is developing ZK-based privacy solutions (e.g., ZKsync’s Prividium, per-issuer ZK rollups for PII privacy) that are architecturally compatible with our model. The key point is that our model is strictly superior to the intermediary model for stockholder inspection rights under DGCL §220: because the stock ledger is onchain and readable, a stockholder demanding inspection can actually inspect it. In Canton’s architecture, by contrast, no single node holds the complete ownership graph, and reconstructing it requires the cooperation of every participant node—which is just the existing intermediary-mediated inspection process on more expensive infrastructure.

“What if there’s a smart contract bug? The stock ledger gets corrupted and you have a legal crisis.”

This is exactly what our Material Adverse Exception Events (MAEE) framework addresses, and no competing standard has anything equivalent. The CyberCorps bylaws explicitly define consensus attacks, contentious hard forks, contract bugs, and regulatory orders as MAEEs, and authorize the corporation to take remedial measures including using administrative privileges to freeze tokens pending resolution. More importantly, the bylaws provide that no issuance, transfer, or other change that arose from or in connection with an MAEE is given legal effect. This is a legal-layer circuit breaker: the law and the code work together, so that if the code fails, the legal framework provides an orderly resolution mechanism. Compare this to ERC-3643’s pause function or ERC-1400’s controller operations—those handle the same scenario mechanically (stop transfers) but without any corresponding legal framework defining what happens next. They freeze the contract but leave the legal consequences undefined.

“Compliance obligations require intermediaries. You can’t just encode compliance in a smart contract and call it done.”

We agree completely, and this is actually one of our central arguments against the competing standards, not against us. Compliance obligations attach to regulated persons and entities (broker-dealers, transfer agents, issuers), not to tokens or smart contracts. A compliance module that returns true or false based on deterministic inputs is not a compliance program—it is a boolean function masquerading as one. ERC-3643’s entire architecture is built on the assumption that encoding compliance checks in the token transfer layer is the right approach, when in reality a transfer agent cannot satisfy its obligations under Exchange Act §17A by deferring to a canTransfer() response. The CyberCorps model does not try to encode compliance in the token. Instead, the issuer retains the governance powers DGCL requires (enforce transfer restrictions, conduct corporate actions, maintain stockholder records) and exercises them through the same onchain system via BorgAuth. Transfer restrictions on cyberCERTs are the DGCL §202 restrictions themselves, implemented by the smart contract rejecting noncompliant transactions, because the smart contract IS the stock ledger and rejecting the transaction IS the issuer’s refusal to register the transfer.

“Isn’t MetaLeX just becoming the new intermediary?”

This is probably the most important architectural question, and we’ve designed specifically against it. The co-approval upgrade model is the key: we publish new contract implementations, but each issuer (each cyberCORP) decides whether and when to adopt them. No unilateral pushes. No forced migrations. An issuer can stay on its original contracts indefinitely. If the protocol operator could unilaterally modify the stock ledger contracts, it would effectively be a transfer agent with admin keys, and we’d have recreated the intermediary dependency the architecture is designed to eliminate. We are a protocol steward, not a securities intermediary. This matters for regulatory classification (we are not a transfer agent or broker-dealer), for the trust model (issuers and investors don’t need to trust MetaLeX with ongoing control over their securities), and for the “walkaway test”: if MetaLeX disappeared tomorrow, every cyberCORP and its securities would continue to function on the blockchain. No vendor lock-in.

“Doesn’t the ERC-721 model mean every stock transfer is an NFT trade? That’s terrible UX for liquid securities.”

This is what cyberSCRIPs solve. The dual-token model separates the legal record (ERC-721 cyberCERTs for the stock ledger) from the liquid composable layer (ERC-20 cyberSCRIPs for DeFi and trading). A stockholder who wants liquidity scripifies their cyberCERT into fungible ERC-20 tokens, trades or deploys them freely, and the buyer can de-scripify back into a registered cyberCERT when they want full stockholder rights. Think of the stablecoin model: your average USDC holder has no direct rights against Circle, but qualifying clients can redeem 1:1. The cyberCERT layer provides legal fidelity and per-share metadata; the cyberSCRIP layer provides liquidity and composability. No other standard offers both. Competing standards offer only fungible tokens (which lack metadata) or only NFTs (which lack liquidity). We solved the tradeoff.

“This only works for Delaware corporations. What about other jurisdictions?”

Delaware is the right starting point: it governs over 65% of Fortune 500 companies, the vast majority of venture-backed startups, and has the most developed body of corporate law and case law in the United States. But we’re not limited to it. Wyoming’s Business Corporation Act (§17-16-604) expressly authorizes scrip representing fractional shares. Other U.S. states and international jurisdictions are developing their own digital asset corporate frameworks. Our architecture is designed to be jurisdiction-adaptable: the legal instruments (bylaws, COI) are the layer that maps the protocol to a specific jurisdiction’s corporate law, and those can be re-authored for different jurisdictions while the smart contract infrastructure remains the same. Delaware is the beachhead, not the ceiling.

“You’re a seed-stage company competing against platforms with hundreds of millions in AUM. What’s your realistic path to scale?”

Our distribution advantage is that we don’t need to sell enterprise contracts. A founder connects a wallet, configures a cyberCORP, and launches a cyberRaise. There is no sales call, no onboarding process, no platform KYC gate. This permissionless model means our growth is bottlenecked by awareness and product quality, not by sales team capacity. Our founder’s @lex_node persona is already the distribution channel for crypto-native venture fundraising—the audience that would use cyberRaise is largely the audience already following Gabriel’s work. The Pump.fun Community SAFE pilot, if successful, opens a second viral channel: every token community with a viable project becomes a potential cyberRaise customer, and those communities bring their own distribution. We don’t need to compete for BlackRock; we need to win the crypto-native private market, which is underserved, growing, and architecturally aligned with everything we’ve built.

“What happens when the SEC decides that onchain stock ledgers need specific regulatory treatment?”

We welcome it. The SEC’s January 2026 joint staff statement already validates our model by distinguishing issuer-sponsored tokenization from third-party models and emphasizing that economic substance governs legal treatment regardless of format. If new rules emerge specifically addressing onchain stock ledgers, we are the best-positioned protocol to comply, because our architecture already maps directly to the statutory requirements. We’re not doing something the SEC hasn’t contemplated—DGCL §224 has permitted electronic stock ledgers since 2017, and our bylaws are an implementation of that existing authority. The real regulatory risk sits with protocols that pretend their tokens are something they’re not: platforms where the token says “tokenized security” but the legal reality is an intermediary-controlled database with a blockchain notification layer. When the SEC looks closely at those architectures, the gap between marketing and substance is where the enforcement risk lives.
