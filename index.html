2.2 But the Entire Market Is Built Wrong

Here is the problem: virtually every dollar of this market rests on a fundamental architectural error. The onchain token is not the security—it is a pointer to an offchain record maintained by a transfer agent, custodian, or proprietary database. We coined the term ”slop tokenization” to describe this phenomenon. The taxonomy is precise: instruction tokens (messages to an intermediary the intermediary can ignore), receipt tokens (acknowledgments of offchain events), and souvenir tokens (no legal operative function whatsoever). In all three cases, strip away the blockchain and the underlying ownership structure is unchanged. If the blockchain isn’t performing essential work, why do you need one?

Our immediate focus is the private market securities segment: venture-stage equity (SAFEs, preferred stock, common stock), SPV interests, and token-equity hybrid instruments. Global venture capital deal volume exceeded $300 billion in 2024, with tens of thousands of SAFE and preferred stock transactions per year, virtually all settling through PDF signatures, wire transfers, and manual cap table management on Carta. Think of us as Carta and Nasdaq Private Market in full symbiosis—but crypto native, onchain, and liquid. For crypto native companies, where founders and investors already have wallet infrastructure, understand self-custody, and prefer permissionless tooling, we offer a fundamentally better way to raise and manage capital.

4. Our Solution: Securities That Know What They Are

3.1 The Entity-First Architecture

We invert the approach taken by every competing standard. Rather than starting from a token standard and adding compliance features, CyberCorps starts from Delaware General Corporation Law and builds token architecture to satisfy its requirements. The organizing principle is that every component in the system should know its relationship to the others—and should be able to act on that knowledge.

The protocol’s root contract is the cyberCORP—an onchain representation of the corporate entity itself, storing the legal entity’s name, type, jurisdiction, governance information, and officer identities. The cyberCORP knows what entity it is, what securities it has authorized, and what governance rules apply to corporate actions. Securities are issued from this entity contract, creating a formal onchain link between token and issuer that no competing standard provides.

3.2 The Dual-Token Model

3.2.1 cyberCERTs (ERC-721 Stock Ledger Entry Tokens)

Each cyberCERT is an ERC-721 NFT that functions as an entry on the corporation’s onchain stock ledger. It encodes everything a stock ledger entry requires under DGCL §§158 and 219: stockholder name, number/class/series of shares, authorizing officer signatures, purchase price, voting power, valuation cap, transfer restriction legends (satisfying §202), endorsement history, legal agreement URIs, and a fully onchain Base64-encoded SVG visualization. A cyberCERT knows what entity issued it, what series it belongs to, what terms govern that series, what transfer restrictions apply, and what its acquisition date and price were. Anyone buying the security can consult this single onchain source of truth to understand exactly what they are acquiring.

Critically, NFT transfer alone does not change registered ownership—metadata must be explicitly mutated, maintaining the distinction between token possession and registered ownership that corporate law requires.

3.2.2 cyberSCRIPs (ERC-20 Scrip Tokens)

cyberSCRIPs are ERC-20 fungible tokens generated from cyberCERTs via scripification and convertible back via de-scripification. They are the liquid, DeFi-compatible layer—usable in AMMs, vesting protocols, and as collateral—while cyberCERTs remain the canonical ownership record. Authorized under DGCL §155, Scrip Tokens are explicitly not stock of the corporation—they are conditional claims on underlying cyberCERTs, convertible back into registered stock when conditions are met.

This dual-token model resolves the fundamental tension between legal fidelity and DeFi composability. A cyberCERT holder can scripify their certificate, deposit the resulting cyberSCRIPs into a lending protocol as collateral, and the lender obtains token-control of a fungible ERC-20 without admin-key override risk. Think of it as the stablecoin model applied to equity: USDC holders have no rights per se, but qualifying clients of Circle can redeem 1:1 for dollars.

3.3 The Constitutive Difference

This is the load-bearing argument. In the CyberCorps model, the corporation’s bylaws and Certificate of Incorporation explicitly designate the onchain system as the official stock ledger under DGCL §224. The token doesn’t merely represent a stock ledger entry; it IS the stock ledger entry. Holding it makes you the stockholder of record. The governing legal instruments fuse the onchain state to the legal reality, so that the smart contract system is performing real legal work, not mirroring an offchain record.

Every competing architecture maintains two ledgers that must be kept in sync, proving the chain isn’t the source of truth. We eliminate the reconciliation layer by making the chain the ledger in the governing documents. Transfer restrictions encoded in our smart contracts are doing real work—they are not redundant to an offchain compliance process, because there is no separate offchain ledger serving as the system of record.

3.4 The Fundamental Move: Signing Authority = Spending Authority

Every other corporate deal system separates signing an agreement from wiring money. The gap between the two is where deals fall apart, where counterparty risk lives, and where intermediaries extract fees. Public key cryptography collapses this gap: the same private key that signs a legal agreement can authorize a payment transaction. The signing authority IS the spending authority. We call this design space cybernetic law.

Atomic sign-and-close: In cyberRaise, an investor signs the legal document and deposits funds into escrow in a single transaction. The counterparty risk window is zero. Self-executing escrow: The LeXscroW contract knows its governing terms and automatically releases funds upon acceptance or returns them if the round closes without allocation. No escrow agent required.

cyberRaise is the first product built on this foundation. The vision is much larger: a full corporate lifecycle—found, fund, manage, trade—where every legal act and every economic act are fused into a single programmable system. We call it CorpFi.

12. Frequently Asked Questions

We hear smart, skeptical questions regularly. Here are our honest answers.

”Hasn’t this been tried before? STOs failed in 2017–2019.”

Yes, and for a specific reason: the first generation started from the token and retrofitted legal compliance. Those tokens carried no legal content and duplicated work the transfer agent was already doing offchain. We start from the opposite end: Delaware corporate law first, token architecture second. CyberCorps doesn’t add a blockchain layer on top of the intermediary chain—it replaces the intermediary chain by making the blockchain the legally authoritative stock ledger. Different product, not a retry.

”Securitize has billions in AUM and BlackRock. How do you compete?”

We don’t compete for the same customers yet. Securitize serves institutional fund products where intermediary infrastructure is a feature. Our target is crypto native private companies where founders already have wallets and prefer permissionless tooling. Securitize’s architecture has a structural ceiling: its tokens carry no per-share metadata, its DS Protocol retains god-mode admin keys that make tokens unsuitable for DeFi collateral, and every new DeFi integration requires individual transfer agent approval—a bottleneck that scales linearly.

”Doesn’t putting stockholder names on a public blockchain create privacy issues?”

Our protocol supports encrypted metadata so PII is only visible to authorized parties. Pre-IPO cyberCERTs can be soulbound, limiting onchain visibility. The broader ecosystem is developing ZK-based privacy solutions architecturally compatible with our model. Our approach is actually superior for stockholder inspection rights under DGCL §220: the stock ledger is onchain and readable, unlike Canton’s architecture where no single node holds the complete ownership graph.

”What if there’s a smart contract bug?”

Our Material Adverse Exception Events (MAEE) framework addresses this—no competing standard has anything equivalent. The CyberCorps bylaws explicitly define consensus attacks, hard forks, contract bugs, and regulatory orders as MAEEs, and provide that no change arising from an MAEE is given legal effect. This is a legal-layer circuit breaker: if the code fails, the legal framework provides an orderly resolution mechanism.

”Isn’t MetaLeX just becoming the new intermediary?”

The co-approval upgrade model is the key: we publish new contract implementations, but each cyberCORP decides whether to adopt them. No unilateral pushes, no forced migrations. We are a protocol steward, not a securities intermediary. The walkaway test: if MetaLeX disappeared tomorrow, every cyberCORP and its securities would continue to function on the blockchain.

”This only works for Delaware. What about other jurisdictions?”

Delaware is the right starting point: it governs over 65% of Fortune 500 companies and the vast majority of venture-backed startups. But our architecture is jurisdiction-adaptable: the legal instruments (bylaws, COI) are the layer that maps the protocol to a specific jurisdiction’s corporate law, and those can be re-authored while the smart contract infrastructure remains the same. Delaware is the beachhead, not the ceiling.
